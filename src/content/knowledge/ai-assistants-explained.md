Everyone is talking about AI assistants. Your board member wants to know if you should use ChatGPT. A vendor is pitching you a "custom AI solution." And somewhere in your building, a staff member is already using one on their own to draft emails.

This piece is a plain-English guide to what AI assistants are, what they're good at, what they're not, and how a small organization might actually deploy one responsibly.

## What an AI assistant actually is

At the core, a modern AI assistant is a language model—a system trained on a very large amount of text, which makes it good at generating fluent, plausible responses to text prompts.

The most important word there is *plausible*. The system doesn't look things up or reason from facts the way a person does. It generates text that statistically fits the context. This means it can be wrong with great confidence.

The best practical AI assistants for organizational use are **grounded**: they work from a specific set of documents you control, rather than from general training. This matters because it means answers are traceable. If the assistant says "the deadline for the spring scholarship is March 15," you can verify that against the actual document it drew from. If it hallucinates a deadline that isn't in any document, a well-built system can detect and flag that.

## What they're actually good at

AI assistants are most useful in narrow, high-repetition tasks:

- Answering a specific set of common questions using your documents (FAQs, eligibility rules, event details)
- Summarizing documents or meeting notes into a standard format
- Routing or classifying incoming requests by type before a human reviews them
- Drafting routine communications from a template

They're least useful when the stakes are high, the information changes frequently, or the answer requires judgment rather than retrieval.

## What to get right before you build

Before deploying an assistant, I work with clients to answer three questions:

1. **What is the exact task?** "Answer questions" is too broad. "Answer questions about scholarship eligibility from our 2026 guidelines" is specific enough to build and test.
2. **What does good look like?** We need to be able to evaluate whether the assistant is doing its job. That usually means a set of test questions with correct answers.
3. **What are the failure modes?** What happens when the assistant gets something wrong? Is there a human review step? A way for users to flag a bad answer?

The third question is the most important. A well-designed AI assistant is one where errors are visible and recoverable—not one where confidence masks mistakes.

## A realistic starting point

For most small organizations, the right first project is an "FAQ pilot": one narrow topic, grounded in one document or a small set of documents, with a human reviewing flagged responses. You learn what the system can and can't do, you build intuition for the failure modes, and you have something real to show.
