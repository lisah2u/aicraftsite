Almost every small organization I work with is managing something important in a spreadsheet—and usually more than one. Donors here, programs there, grant deadlines in a third tab, and volunteer hours in a file someone emailed around six months ago.

The spreadsheets contain real, hard-won information. But the information is hard to use because it's scattered, inconsistently formatted, and requires someone to manually combine it before it tells a story.

The answer isn't a big analytics platform. It's clarity about the one or two questions you're trying to answer, followed by the simplest possible view.

## Start with a question, not a tool

Most dashboard projects fail not because the technology is wrong, but because the goals are too vague. "Better visibility into our programs" is not a goal—it's a feeling. 

A good starting question sounds like:
- "How many people applied to our scholarship program this year, and what percentage completed it?"
- "Which of our grants are within 90 days of expiring, and which ones require a final report?"
- "What are our top three service request categories this month?"

Notice these have a specific subject, a timeframe, and an implicit decision they support. A dashboard that answers one of these clearly is worth more than one that shows twenty things vaguely.

## Cleaning before building

The fastest way to make a dashboard useless is to build it on unreliable data. Before visualizing anything, I spend time on the underlying records: making sure names are spelled consistently, dates are in the same format, categories aren't duplicated across three columns, and totals actually add up.

This cleanup work isn't glamorous, but it's what makes the dashboard trustworthy. A board member who catches an obvious error in a chart loses confidence in everything else on it.

## The simplest useful thing

Once the question is clear and the data is reasonably clean, a lightweight dashboard can often be built in a few days. Depending on your team's existing tools, it might live in Google Looker Studio connected to a Sheets document, in a simple web page that reads a CSV, or in a small data app.

The key is matching the complexity of the solution to the actual need. A small nonprofit doesn't need Tableau. It needs one chart its executive director can look at on Monday morning and know whether the week is on track.
